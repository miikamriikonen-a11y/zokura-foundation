## I. Introduction

This document presents a structured comparison of the world's major religious and philosophical traditions. The purpose is not to rank or judge, but to illuminate the shared truths and meaningful differences across humanity's attempts to answer the deepest questions: *Who are we? Why are we here? How should we live?*

The traditions covered:

1. Christianity (Kristinusko)
2. Islam
3. Judaism (Juutalaisuus)
4. Hinduism (Hindulaisuus)
5. Buddhism (Buddhalaisuus)
6. Sikhism (Sikhiläisyys)

8. Shinto (Šintolaisuus)
9. Indigenous & Animist Traditions (Luonnonuskonnot)
10. Zoroastrianism (Zarathustralaisuus)

## II. Core Teachings — Side by Side

### 1. Christianity (est. ~30 CE)

- **Followers:** ~2.4 billion
- **Core text:** Bible (Old and New Testaments)
- **Central teaching:** God is love. Salvation through faith in Jesus Christ, who is both divine and human. The Kingdom of God is within and among you.
- **Golden Rule:** "Do unto others as you would have them do unto you." (Matthew 7:12)
- **On truth:** "And you will know the truth, and the truth will set you free." (John 8:32)
- **On the poor:** "Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven." (Matthew 5:3)
- **Key concept:** Grace — unearned love, freely given.
- **Branches:** Catholic, Orthodox, Protestant (thousands of denominations)

### 2. Islam (est. ~610 CE)

- **Followers:** ~1.9 billion
- **Core text:** Quran; Hadith (sayings of Prophet Muhammad)
- **Central teaching:** There is no god but God (Allah), and Muhammad is His messenger. Submission to God's will brings peace. Justice, mercy, and compassion are divine commands.
- **Golden Rule:** "None of you truly believes until he wishes for his brother what he wishes for himself." (Hadith, Bukhari & Muslim)
- **On truth:** "O you who believe, stand firmly for justice, as witnesses to God, even if it be against yourselves." (Quran 4:135)
- **On the poor:** Zakat (obligatory charity) is one of the Five Pillars.
- **Key concept:** Tawhid — the absolute oneness of God.
- **Branches:** Sunni, Shia, Sufi (mystical tradition)

### 3. Judaism (est. ~2000 BCE)

- **Followers:** ~15 million
- **Core text:** Torah (Tanakh); Talmud
- **Central teaching:** One God, creator of all. A covenant between God and the Jewish people. The purpose of life is to repair the world (Tikkun Olam) through righteous action.
- **Golden Rule:** "What is hateful to you, do not do to your neighbor. That is the whole Torah; the rest is commentary." (Hillel, Talmud, Shabbat 31a)
- **On truth:** "The seal of the Holy One, blessed be He, is truth." (Talmud, Shabbat 55a)
- **On the poor:** Tzedakah (justice/charity) is an obligation, not a choice.
- **Key concept:** Tikkun Olam — repairing the world through righteous deeds.
- **Branches:** Orthodox, Conservative, Reform, Reconstructionist

### 4. Hinduism (est. ~1500 BCE, roots older)

- **Followers:** ~1.2 billion
- **Core texts:** Vedas, Upanishads, Bhagavad Gita, Mahabharata, Ramayana
- **Central teaching:** Brahman is the ultimate reality — infinite, eternal, unchanging. The individual soul (Atman) is one with Brahman. Life is a cycle of birth, death, and rebirth (samsara), governed by karma (action and consequence). Liberation (moksha) is the goal.
- **Golden Rule:** "One should never do that to another which one regards as injurious to one's own self." (Mahabharata, Anusasana Parva 113.8)
- **On truth:** "Satyam eva jayate" — Truth alone triumphs. (Mundaka Upanishad 3.1.6)
- **On the poor:** Dana (generosity) is a core dharmic duty.
- **Key concept:** Dharma — cosmic order, moral duty, righteous living.
- **Branches:** Vaishnavism, Shaivism, Shaktism, Smartism (and many more)

### 5. Buddhism (est. ~500 BCE)

- **Followers:** ~500 million
- **Core texts:** Tripitaka (Pali Canon); Mahayana Sutras; Tibetan Canon
- **Central teaching:** Life involves suffering (dukkha). Suffering arises from attachment and ignorance. Suffering can end. The path to the end of suffering is the Noble Eightfold Path: right view, right intention, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, right concentration.
- **Golden Rule:** "Hurt not others in ways that you yourself would find hurtful." (Udanavarga 5:18)
- **On truth:** "Three things cannot be long hidden: the sun, the moon, and the truth." (attributed to the Buddha)
- **On the poor:** Compassion (karuna) for all beings is foundational.
- **Key concept:** Dependent origination — everything arises in dependence upon conditions. Nothing exists in isolation.
- **Branches:** Theravada, Mahayana, Vajrayana (Tibetan)

### 6. Sikhism (est. ~1469 CE)

- **Followers:** ~30 million
- **Core text:** Guru Granth Sahib
- **Central teaching:** One God (Ik Onkar), formless and eternal. All human beings are equal. Live honestly, work hard, share with others. Remember God in all things.
- **Golden Rule:** "I am not the born; how can there be either birth or death for me? [...] As you see me, so I am." — "As you deem others, so deem yourself." (Guru Granth Sahib)
- **On truth:** "Truth is the highest virtue, but higher still is truthful living." (Guru Nanak, Guru Granth Sahib, p. 62)
- **On the poor:** Langar — free community kitchen open to all, regardless of caste, creed, or status.
- **Key concept:** Seva — selfless service to humanity as worship.
- **Branches:** Primarily unified; Namdhari, Nirankari as minor movements

### 7. Taoism (est. ~400 BCE)

- **Followers:** ~12 million (formal); cultural influence on hundreds of millions
- **Core texts:** Tao Te Ching (Laozi); Zhuangzi
- **Central teaching:** The Tao (道, the Way) is the fundamental, nameless force underlying all existence. Live in harmony with the Tao through simplicity, humility, and non-action (wu wei). The universe balances itself.
- **Golden Rule:** "Regard your neighbor's gain as your own gain, and your neighbor's loss as your own loss." (Tai Shang Kan Ying Pien)
- **On truth:** "The Tao that can be told is not the eternal Tao." (Tao Te Ching, Ch. 1)
- **On the poor:** "The sage has no fixed mind; he takes the mind of the people as his mind." (Tao Te Ching, Ch. 49)
- **Key concept:** Wu wei (無為) — effortless action; not forcing, but flowing.
- **Connection to Zokura:** Tai Ji (太極) — the supreme ultimate, the interplay of yin and yang — is directly referenced in the Zokura Manifesto as a core principle.

### 8. Shinto (est. prehistoric, formalized ~700 CE)

- **Followers:** ~4 million (formal); ~70% of Japanese participate in Shinto practices
- **Core texts:** Kojiki; Nihon Shoki (historical, not doctrinal)
- **Central teaching:** Kami (spirits/gods) exist in all things — mountains, rivers, trees, ancestors. Purity (harae) and harmony with nature and community are paramount. There is no single creator god and no fixed doctrine.
- **Golden Rule:** "Be charitable to all beings; love is the representative of God." (Ko-ji-ki Hachiman Kasuga)
- **On truth:** Sincerity (makoto) is the highest virtue.
- **On the poor:** Communal harmony; the group cares for its members.
- **Key concept:** Musubi — the creative, interconnecting power of the universe.
- **Connection to Zokura:** The Japanese cultural elements in Zokura (Shokunin, Kunnia/名誉, Samurai, Nakama) have roots in Shinto's reverence for craft, honor, and communal bonds.

### 9. Indigenous & Animist Traditions (est. prehistoric)

- **Followers:** ~300+ million across all continents
- **Core texts:** Oral traditions, song, ceremony, landscape
- **Central teaching:** All things are alive and interconnected — rocks, rivers, animals, ancestors, sky. The land is sacred. Reciprocity with nature is survival. Elders carry wisdom. Community is everything.
- **Golden Rule:** "All things are connected. Whatever befalls the earth, befalls the children of the earth." (attributed to Chief Seattle, Suquamish)
- **On truth:** Truth is lived, not written. It is carried in stories, ceremonies, and relationships.
- **On the poor:** The concept of individual poverty is foreign; the community provides.
- **Key concept:** Reciprocity — take only what you need; give back what you can.
- **Note:** This is not a single religion but a family of thousands of traditions. Generalizations are inherently reductive.

### 10. Zoroastrianism (est. ~1500–1000 BCE)

- **Followers:** ~100,000–200,000
- **Core text:** Avesta (Gathas are the oldest layer)
- **Central teaching:** One supreme God (Ahura Mazda) created the world. Existence is a cosmic struggle between truth (Asha) and falsehood (Druj). Humans must choose truth. Good thoughts, good words, good deeds.
- **Golden Rule:** "That nature alone is good which refrains from doing to another whatsoever is not good for itself." (Dadestan-i Dinik 94:5)
- **On truth:** Asha — truth and cosmic order — is the highest principle.
- **On the poor:** Charity and care for others are sacred duties.
- **Key concept:** Free will — each person must choose between truth and falsehood.
- **Historical note:** Zoroastrianism profoundly influenced Judaism, Christianity, and Islam (concepts of heaven/hell, final judgment, messianic figures, cosmic dualism).

## IV. The Universal Thread

Every tradition examined here shares the following core principles:

1. **Treat others as you wish to be treated.** — Present in every single tradition. No exceptions.
2. **Truth matters.** — Every tradition elevates truth, honesty, or sincerity as a foundational virtue.
3. **Compassion is non-negotiable.** — Whether called love, mercy, karuna, rahma, chesed, or daya — the imperative to care for others is universal.
4. **Care for the vulnerable.** — Every tradition demands attention to those who suffer, whether through obligatory charity, communal sharing, or selfless service.
5. **Humility before the infinite.** — Whether the infinite is called God, Brahman, Tao, or simply the mystery of existence, every tradition teaches that humans are not the center.

These five principles are not coincidences. They are convergent solutions to the same problem: *how do conscious beings live together without destroying each other and themselves?*

## V. The Honest Differences

To pretend all religions say the same thing would be dishonest. Key divergences:

- **The nature of God:** Ranges from strict monotheism (Islam, Sikhism) to no-god frameworks (Buddhism, Taoism) to complex multi-layered theologies (Hinduism, Shinto). These are genuinely different metaphysical claims.
- **What happens after death:** Reincarnation/rebirth cycles (Hinduism, Buddhism, Sikhism) vs. linear afterlife (Christianity, Islam, Zoroastrianism) vs. focus on this life (Judaism, Taoism, Indigenous traditions). These shape how people live.
- **Authority and scripture:** Some traditions center on a single revealed text (Quran, Torah); others have vast libraries of texts with no single canon (Hinduism); others have no texts at all (Indigenous traditions).
- **Exclusivity:** Some traditions claim to be the only path to salvation; others explicitly accept multiple paths. This is perhaps the deepest and most consequential difference.
- **The individual vs. the collective:** Western monotheisms tend to emphasize individual salvation and individual moral responsibility. Eastern and Indigenous traditions tend to emphasize interconnection, communal harmony, and the dissolution of the separate self.

These differences are real and should be respected, not dissolved into false equivalence.

## VI. Historical Cross-Pollination

Religions did not develop in isolation. Key influences:

- **Zoroastrianism → Judaism → Christianity → Islam:** The concepts of heaven and hell, final judgment, resurrection, angels, and a cosmic battle between good and evil trace from Zoroastrian theology into the Abrahamic traditions during and after the Babylonian Exile (6th century BCE).
- **Hinduism → Buddhism → East Asian traditions:** The Buddha was born Hindu. Buddhist concepts of karma, meditation, and ethics spread to China, Japan, Korea, and Southeast Asia, blending with local Taoism, Confucianism, and Shinto.
- **Greek philosophy → Christianity → Islam:** Platonic and Aristotelian thought deeply shaped Christian theology (Augustine, Aquinas) and Islamic philosophy (Al-Farabi, Ibn Sina, Ibn Rushd).
- **Hinduism ↔ Sikhism ↔ Islam:** Sikhism emerged in the Punjab as a distinct tradition engaging with both Hindu and Islamic thought. Guru Nanak's teachings explicitly transcend both: "There is no Hindu, there is no Muslim."
- **Indigenous ↔ All:** Indigenous ecological wisdom is increasingly recognized as essential knowledge for addressing the climate crisis — a convergence with modern science that the formalized religions are still catching up to.

## VII. Concluding Analysis

### What the evidence shows

The comparative study of religions reveals a remarkable pattern: **the deeper you go into any tradition, the more it converges with the others at the level of ethics and lived practice.** The surface differences — rituals, cosmologies, institutional structures — are real and important, but they are surface. The root system is shared.

This is not a sentimental claim. It is an empirical observation. When Hillel says "the rest is commentary," when the Buddha teaches the end of suffering through compassion, when Jesus says "love your neighbor," when the Quran commands justice even against yourself, when Guru Nanak feeds everyone regardless of caste, when Laozi teaches flowing with rather than forcing against — they are describing the same underlying recognition:

**Reality is interconnected. Compassion is rational. Truth is non-negotiable. The vulnerable must be protected.**

### The problem is not religion

The historical record shows that religion has been used to justify conquest, slavery, oppression, and genocide. But the historical record also shows that science, nationalism, and secular ideologies have done the same. The variable is not the system of thought — it is the human tendency to use any system of thought as a weapon when power, fear, or greed take over.

Every tradition examined here contains within itself the tools to critique its own abuses. The prophetic tradition in Judaism and Christianity, the concept of fitna (civil strife) in Islam, the Buddhist emphasis on non-attachment to views, the Taoist warning against forcing — these are built-in corrective mechanisms. The tragedy is how often they are ignored.

### What this means for Zokura

The Zokura Manifesto states: **"Rakkaus ja Totuus. Aina."** — Love and Truth. Always.

This is not a religious statement. But it is a statement that every religion examined here would recognize. It is the universal thread, stripped of cosmology, ritual, and institutional structure, reduced to its simplest form.

The Manifesto also states: **"Aina pienemmän puolella."** — Always on the side of the smaller one.

This too is universal. Every tradition demands care for the vulnerable. Every tradition fails at it. The difference between a tradition and a lived ethic is whether you actually do it.

Occam's Razor, as applied in the Manifesto (Section XVI), cuts to the same conclusion that the comparative study of religions reaches: **the simplest explanation that accounts for all the data — all the convergence, all the shared ethics, all the independent discoveries of the same truths — is that these truths are real.** Not because a god said so. Not because a text says so. Because reality itself is structured in a way that rewards compassion, punishes cruelty, and tends toward connection.

Or, as the Manifesto puts it: the simplest foundation for everything is love.
